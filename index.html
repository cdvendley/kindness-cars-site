Our story

How it started

Fisher visits Seattle Children’s Hospital more than most kids. Since his very first appointment, he has always brought along a collection of toy cars to play with.

Over time, he started sharing those cars with other kids he met and even giving away a few from his own collection. Eventually, he came up with a new idea: bring brand-new cars to hand out in the waiting room.

What began as one child’s instinct to share has grown into the Kindness Cars Project.

What we do

Growing one small act of kindness at a time

We are still in the early days. Friends and family have been donating new toy cars, and Fisher has been handing them out during his hospital visits. On his last visit alone, he gave away more than 20 cars.

We have seen how a simple spontaneous gift can bring smiles, ease tension, and create a small moment of connection in an unfamiliar setting. We have also seen that the kids doing the giving often enjoy it just as much as the kids receiving.

Fisher is the visionary behind this idea. His family is here to support him as we keep it going and grow it beyond our small start.

Start your own gifting practice

Simple, thoughtful, and easy to begin

It is easy to get started. Think of something small and broadly loved by kids. Fisher is a car nut, so he chooses toy cars, but crayons, little animal figures, trinkets, or something else you would genuinely enjoy sharing can work too.

Be mindful of the setting. Noisy, messy, or sticky gifts are probably not the best fit for a waiting room or clinic. Ask friends and family to help you gather a few simple items, then bring them along the next time you have an appointment.

Fisher likes to pick out a car, approach a child with their parent nearby, and say, “Excuse me, would you like to have a car?” Kids almost always say yes. It can feel a little awkward the first couple of times, but before long you will find your own style.

We do not worry too much about whether a child is young or old, the patient or a sibling. Some of the biggest reactions come from the biggest kids. The key is to keep the interaction short, simple, and kind.
